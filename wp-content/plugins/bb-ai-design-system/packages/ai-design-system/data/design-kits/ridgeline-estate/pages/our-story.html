Chapter One · 1978

Harold and June
and a Hillside

Harold Callahan was a high school geology teacher in Sacramento. June was a nurse. In the spring of 1977 they drove up Highway 29 on a long weekend, took a wrong turn off the Silverado Trail, and found themselves on a washboard road climbing the eastern ridge of the valley.

The property at the top had been a walnut orchard, abandoned for a decade. The trees were gone. What was left was forty-six acres of rocky, south-facing hillside and a stone barn that had been standing since before Prohibition. They bought it in the fall for $88,000.

The first Cabernet vines went in the ground in October of '78 — eleven rows, planted by hand with a neighbor's tractor and a borrowed auger. June kept the books on a legal pad in the kitchen. Harold taught through the first three vintages to keep the lights on. They did not take a salary from the winery until 1986.

The Site

A Hillside
That Earns Its Fruit

We sit 840 feet above the valley floor, on a south-southwest-facing slope of volcanic soil — reddish, rocky, thin enough that the vines have to work for their water. That stress is the whole point. Hillside fruit ripens slower and concentrates deeper than anything grown on the flats.

Fog rolls up from the bay most summer mornings and holds the vines cool past ten. By two in the afternoon the sun is direct and hard. The day ends slow, the way a Cabernet wants — a long warm evening instead of a sudden drop.

We dry-farm the oldest eighteen acres. The younger blocks take water twice a season. That's it.

The Continuity

Three Generations
of Honest Wine

The estate has changed hands twice in forty-seven years — both times across a kitchen table. The second generation inherited the books. The third inherited the dirt under their fingernails. Nothing has been sold to outside ownership. Nothing will be.

1978 – 2002 First Generation

Planting the Hillside

Harold & June Callahan

Built the stone winery from the walls up. Planted the first eighteen acres, bottled the first vintage in 1981, and kept the books by hand until the day they handed over the keys.

2002 – 2019 Second Generation

Steadying the Work

Tom & Clara Callahan

Doubled acreage to forty-six. Replanted the original Cabernet blocks on phylloxera-resistant rootstock. Converted the barrel program to French oak and held the line on estate-grown only.

2019 – Today Third Generation

Carrying Forward

James & Eleanor Callahan

James makes the wine. Eleanor runs the estate and hospitality. The rows are still picked by hand. The wines still bottle when they are ready — not when the calendar says so.

The Way We've
Always Done It

From Vine to Bottle

Four steps. The same four steps we have followed since the first vintage. There is no secret here, and there is nothing new. There is only the patience to do it the way it deserves.

01 In the Rows

Picked by Hand

Every cluster is cut by hand into a ten-pound bin, sorted once in the row and again on the crush pad. No mechanical harvesters have ever run this hillside.

02 In the Cellar

Native Ferments

We let the fruit start on its own. Wild yeast, open tops, punched down by hand twice a day for about three weeks. No enzymes, no color additions, no shortcuts.

03 In the Cave

French Oak, Unhurried

Twenty-two months in French oak, roughly a third of the barrels new. The cave stays at fifty-eight degrees year-round, so the wine takes its time and we take ours.

04 At the Line

Bottled When Ready

The winemaker calls it. If the wine needs another six months, it gets another six months. We have never released a bottle we didn't pour for ourselves first.
